# Idea Critic: The Final Stress Test

## Purpose

This is the adversarial review. After the optimism of idea generation and the structured analysis of the deep dive, we now ask the hardest question: **"Why will this NOT work?"**

Every successful entrepreneur has a well-developed internal critic. This file IS that critic.

## The Pre-Mortem

Imagine it's 18 months from now. The business has failed. You're shutting it down. Write the post-mortem.

Why did it fail? The most common real reasons:

### 1. The founder gave up
Not because the idea was bad, but because:
- Progress was slower than expected
- The day-to-day work was soul-crushingly boring
- A life event (new job, kid, relationship) changed priorities
- The founder was motivated by the market, not the problem
- Solo founder burnout with no one to share the load

**Question:** On a scale of 1-10, how motivated are you to work on this specific problem for 3 years, even if growth is painfully slow?

### 2. Distribution never clicked
The product worked. Some early users loved it. But:
- No scalable acquisition channel was found
- CAC was too high for the price point
- The target market was hard to reach
- Content/SEO took too long to compound
- Paid acquisition was too expensive

**Question:** Can you describe, RIGHT NOW, exactly how you'd reach 100 potential customers this week? (Not "I'd run ads" — specifically: which platform, what targeting, what message, what budget?)

### 3. The market was too small
The problem was real but:
- Not enough people had it
- People who had it didn't care enough to pay
- The willingness-to-pay was lower than expected
- The market was shrinking, not growing

**Question:** Have you talked to actual potential customers? Not friends and family. Strangers who fit the target profile. What did they say?

### 4. Couldn't differentiate from competitors
Competitors existed and:
- They had more resources (money, team, brand)
- The switching cost was too high for customers
- The differentiation was too subtle for customers to notice
- A larger player added your feature as a minor update

**Question:** If a competitor with 10x your resources decided to copy your product tomorrow — what would you still have that they don't?

### 5. Unit economics didn't work
The business "worked" but:
- Churn was too high (customers left faster than new ones came)
- Support costs ate the margin
- Infrastructure costs scaled faster than revenue
- The price was too low and couldn't be raised

**Question:** What's the absolute minimum monthly price where this business is viable? Would customers actually pay that?

## Cognitive Bias Checklist

When evaluating your own idea, you WILL fall prey to these biases. Check each one:

### Confirmation bias
You seek out information that supports the idea and ignore information that contradicts it.
- **Check:** Have you actively looked for reasons this WON'T work? Have you talked to people who would NOT buy this?

### Survivorship bias
You look at successful companies in the space and think "if they did it, I can too." You don't see the 1,000 that failed.
- **Check:** Can you find examples of companies that tried something similar and FAILED? Why did they fail?

### Dunning-Kruger effect
You underestimate the complexity because you don't know what you don't know.
- **Check:** What are the hardest problems in this space? Have you talked to someone who actually has deep expertise?

### Sunk cost fallacy
You've already spent time/money thinking about this, so you don't want to let go.
- **Check:** If you were starting fresh today with no prior investment, would you still choose this idea?

### Optimism bias
You assume best-case timelines, conversion rates, and growth curves.
- **Check:** What happens if everything takes 3x longer and performs at 50% of your projections?

### Anchoring bias
The first number you heard (market size, price point, growth rate) colors all subsequent thinking.
- **Check:** Where did your assumptions come from? Are they based on real data or things you've read/heard?

## The "Would I Bet My Own Money?" Test

Forget about time. Time is abstract. Think about money.

"Would I take $10,000 of my own savings and put it on this idea delivering $50,000+ in revenue within 18 months?"

If the answer is genuinely yes — good signal.
If you hesitate — listen to that hesitation. It knows something your optimism doesn't.

## The "Tell a Skeptic" Test

Explain the idea to the most skeptical person you know. Not someone who's supportive and encouraging. Someone who asks uncomfortable questions.

If the idea survives a tough conversation with a real skeptic — it has legs.
If you find yourself getting defensive and making excuses — that's data.

## Final Rating

After the full stress test, rate the idea:

| Category | Score (1-10) | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Problem severity | | How badly do customers need this solved? |
| Willingness to pay | | Would they pay YOUR price? |
| Distribution clarity | | Can you describe the exact path to first customers? |
| Competitive defensibility | | What stops others from eating your lunch? |
| Founder motivation | | Will you stick with this when it gets hard? |
| Unit economics | | Does the math work, pessimistically? |
| **TOTAL** | **/60** | |

### Interpretation:
- **45-60:** Strong idea. Proceed to MVP immediately.
- **35-44:** Promising but with gaps. Address the weakest areas before building.
- **25-34:** Weak. Fundamental rework needed, or move on.
- **Below 25:** Dead. The market, the critic, or the math has spoken. Next idea.

## The Most Important Thing

An honest 30 is infinitely more valuable than an inflated 50.

The goal is NOT to justify the idea. The goal is to find the truth. Because the market will find the truth for you — and it charges tuition.

Better to kill a bad idea in 30 minutes than discover it's bad after 6 months of building.

Move fast. Kill fast. The right idea is out there — but you have to clear the bad ones out of the way first.
