# Deep Dive: Analyzing the Survivors

## Purpose

The 1-2 ideas that survived all five filters deserve a deeper look. This is where we move from "could this work?" to "HOW would this work?" — with concrete numbers, real competitors, and an actual plan.

## Section 1: Competitive Landscape

### Direct competitors
List every product that solves the same problem for the same customer. For each:
- Name and URL
- Pricing
- What they do well
- What they do poorly (read their 1-star reviews)
- How big they are (employees, funding, revenue if available)

### Indirect competitors
What do customers use TODAY if your product doesn't exist?
- Spreadsheets? (Which ones? What's in them?)
- Manual processes? (How long do they take?)
- A combination of tools? (Which ones? How are they stitched together?)
- Nothing? (They just live with the pain?)

### Your competitive angle
Why would a customer switch FROM the current solution TO yours? Possible angles:
- **Price:** You're significantly cheaper (dangerous — easy to undercut)
- **Specialization:** You're built for THEIR specific industry (strong — generalists can't match depth)
- **Integration:** You connect to their existing tools (Stripe integration, SSO support, QuickBooks sync)
- **Simplicity:** You do one thing well instead of everything poorly
- **Distribution:** You're where they already are (marketplace, community, platform)

### Competitive moat
What prevents a competitor from copying you in 6 months?
- **Network effects:** More users = more value (marketplaces, communities)
- **Data advantages:** Your product gets better with usage data
- **Integration depth:** Deep integrations are hard to replicate
- **Community/brand:** Trust built over time
- **Switching costs:** Once set up, painful to leave

Be honest. If the answer is "nothing prevents copying" — that's a real risk.

## Section 2: Go-to-Market Strategy

### Phase 1: First 10 customers (Month 1-3)
How do you get your very first paying users?
- Direct outreach (who exactly? list names if possible)
- Community presence (which communities? how will you contribute value before selling?)
- Content that attracts buyers (what search terms? what pain-point content?)

### Phase 2: First 100 customers (Month 3-12)
How do you scale what worked in Phase 1?
- Which channel worked best? Double down.
- Can you build a referral loop? (Do happy customers naturally tell others?)
- Content/SEO flywheel? (Does content compound over time?)
- Partnership opportunities? (Who has your customers and isn't competing with you?)

### Phase 3: First 1,000 customers (Year 2-3)
What changes at scale?
- Do you need a sales team?
- Does the product need to expand scope?
- Are there adjacent markets to enter?
- Can you build platform/ecosystem effects?

### Pricing strategy
- **Anchoring:** What are customers paying for the current solution?
- **Value-based:** What's the ROI of your product? (Time saved × hourly rate, revenue gained, cost avoided)
- **Tiers:** Free/trial → Starter → Pro → Enterprise?
- **Monthly vs. annual:** Annual with discount (better cash flow) or monthly (lower barrier)?

## Section 3: MVP Scope

### The cardinal sin
Building too much before getting it in front of customers. The MVP is NOT a "minimum viable product" — it's a "minimum viable TEST of your riskiest assumption."

### What's the riskiest assumption?
For each idea, identify the ONE thing that, if wrong, kills the entire business. Then build ONLY enough to test that.

Examples:
- "Will landscaping companies pay for route optimization?" → Test: Manual route optimization service (you do it for them) at the target price point
- "Will accountants switch from their current workflow?" → Test: Landing page with clear value prop, measure sign-up rate
- "Can we actually automate this process?" → Test: Prototype the hardest technical piece, ignore everything else

### MVP feature list
Maximum 3-5 features. For each:
- What does it do?
- Why is it essential for the test?
- What's the simplest possible implementation?

Everything else goes in the "NOT in MVP" list. Be ruthless.

### Timeline
- **Week 1-2:** Build core feature
- **Week 3:** Get it in front of 5 potential customers
- **Week 4:** Iterate based on feedback

If the MVP takes more than 4 weeks to build — you're building too much.

## Section 5: Risk Analysis

### Technical risks
- Can this actually be built? What's the hardest technical challenge?
- Are there dependencies on third-party APIs or services?
- Does it require data you don't have access to?

### Market risks
- Is this market growing or shrinking?
- Is there regulatory risk? (New laws that could help or kill the business)
- Could a platform change destroy the business? (API access revoked, algorithm change, etc.)

### Execution risks
- Do you have the skills to build this?
- Can you do this with the time you have available?
- Do you need co-founders or early hires?
- What happens if it takes 2x longer than expected?

### Financial risks
- How long can you fund this before revenue?
- What's the minimum revenue needed to sustain yourself?
- Is there a path to revenue within 6 months?
