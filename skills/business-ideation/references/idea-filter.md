# Idea Filter: The Five Filters That Kill Bad Ideas Early

## Purpose

Most ideas should die. The goal of this filter is NOT to find reasons an idea could work — it's to find reasons it WON'T work. Ideas that survive all five filters deserve deeper analysis. Ideas that fail even one filter should be discarded or fundamentally reworked.

## Filter 1: Problem Validation

**Core question:** Is this a REAL problem that people ALREADY spend money or significant time solving?

### Green flags:
- People are already paying for an inferior solution
- People complain about this problem in forums, communities, Reddit
- You've experienced this problem yourself, repeatedly
- There's a clear "before" (pain) and "after" (relief)

### Red flags:
- "I think people would want this" (you think, but you haven't asked)
- The problem is theoretical, not experienced
- People have the problem but don't care enough to pay for a solution
- The problem only exists if you explain it — real problems don't need explanation

### Kill criterion:
If you can't find 5 real people who have this problem AND are currently spending money or significant time on it — the idea dies here.

## Filter 2: Market Size

**Core question:** Is the market big enough to support a business, but small enough that giants won't crush you?

### The Napkin Math:

```
Example: Landscaping companies in the US with 5-20 crews
~15,000 companies × $50/month × 12 = $9M/year max
```

Is $9M/year enough? For a bootstrapped SaaS with 2-3 people: absolutely. For a VC-backed startup that needs to return 100x: no.

### Sweet spot for bootstrapped:
- TAM (Total Addressable Market): $5M-$100M/year
- Realistic capture: 5-15% in 3-5 years
- Target: $500K-$5M ARR

### Red flags:
- Market is so big that Google/Microsoft will build it as a feature
- Market is so small that even 100% share doesn't support a business
- Market is shrinking (dying industry, regulatory change coming)

### Kill criterion:
If the napkin math doesn't produce at least $1M ARR potential with realistic market capture — the idea dies here.

## Filter 3: Distribution

**Core question:** How do the first 10 customers find you? (Not the first 10,000 — the first 10.)

### Strong distribution channels:
- **You already have access to the customers** (existing network, community, client base)
- **There's a natural aggregation point** (industry conference, subreddit, Facebook group, trade association)
- **Integration/marketplace distribution** (QuickBooks app store, Shopify apps, WordPress plugins)
- **Content/SEO with buyer intent** (people google this problem)
- **Outbound is viable** (you can identify and reach buyers directly)

### Weak distribution channels:
- "We'll do social media marketing" (that's not a channel, that's a hope)
- "Word of mouth" (only works AFTER you have customers)
- "We'll go viral" (not a strategy)
- "Paid ads" (works only if unit economics support it — see Filter 4)

### The landscaping test:
"If I wanted to sell to landscaping companies, how would I reach 100 of them by next week?"
- If you can answer concretely (industry forum, trade show, Facebook group for landscaping business owners, cold-email list from public licensing data) — distribution exists.
- If you can't answer — the idea is missing its most important piece.

### Kill criterion:
If you can't describe a concrete, specific way to get the first 10 paying customers — the idea dies here.

## Filter 4: Unit Economics

**Core question:** Does the math work per customer?

### The formula:
```
LTV (Lifetime Value) = Monthly revenue × Average customer lifespan in months
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = Total sales/marketing cost ÷ New customers acquired
LTV:CAC ratio must be > 3:1 for a healthy business
```

### Example:
```
Monthly price: $50/month
Average lifespan: 24 months
LTV = $50 × 24 = $1,200

CAC via content marketing: ~$200
CAC via paid ads: ~$400
CAC via outbound sales: ~$600

LTV:CAC = $1,200 : $400 = 3:1 ✓
```

### Red flags:
- Price point is too low to support any acquisition cost ($5/month means you need viral/organic only)
- High churn expected (seasonal business, project-based usage)
- Long sales cycle with low contract value
- Requires enterprise sales motion for SMB pricing

### Kill criterion:
If LTV:CAC ratio can't realistically exceed 3:1 — the idea dies here, or the pricing model needs fundamental rework.

## Filter 5: Founder-Idea Fit

**Core question:** Is THIS founder the right person to build THIS specific thing?

### Strong fit:
- You've worked in the industry (you know the customer, the jargon, the real problems)
- You have the technical skills to build v1 yourself
- You have existing relationships with potential customers
- You're motivated by the problem, not just the market
- Your personal constraints match the business model (e.g., bootstrapper lifestyle ↔ niche SaaS)

### Weak fit:
- You picked the idea because the market looks big, not because you understand the customer
- You'd need to hire specialists before you can build anything
- You have no access to the target customer
- You're excited about the technology, not the problem
- The business requires full-time commitment but you can only do evenings

### The 3-year test:
"Would I still want to work on this in 3 years, even if growth is slow?"
If the answer is no — the idea might survive the other filters, but it'll die from founder burnout.

### Kill criterion:
If the founder has no credible advantage over a random person building the same thing — the idea dies here.

## Summary Matrix

For each idea, fill in:

| Filter | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Problem Validation | | Is it real? Are people paying/suffering? |
| Market Size | | Napkin math: TAM and realistic capture |
| Distribution | | First 10 customers — how? |
| Unit Economics | | LTV:CAC > 3:1? |
| Founder Fit | | Why YOU for THIS idea? |

**Scoring:**
- 5 = Strong pass (clear evidence)
- 4 = Likely pass (reasonable assumptions)
- 3 = Uncertain (needs validation)
- 2 = Weak (significant concerns)
- 1 = Fail (deal-breaker)

**Decision:**
- All filters 3+, at least two 4+ → Proceed to deep dive
- Any filter 1 → Idea is dead
- Multiple filters 2 → Idea is wounded, probably dead
