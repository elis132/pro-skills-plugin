# Idea Generation: Find Ideas That Match YOU

## The Principle

Ideas generated in a vacuum ("what's a good SaaS idea?") are worthless. Ideas generated from a specific person's experiences, skills, and assets have a chance.

## Framework 1: Scratch Your Own Itch

Your own problems are gold. You understand them deeply, you know what existing solutions lack, and you are your own first test user.

Questions to ask:
- What was the last thing you googled a solution for and couldn't find a good one?
- What do you do manually in your work that should be automated?
- Which Excel/spreadsheet process do you wish you could eliminate?
- Which SaaS do you pay for but are unhappy with?
- What information do you collect manually that should already exist in one place?

### Example patterns:
"I spend 3 hours a week matching invoices to chart-of-accounts codes in QuickBooks" → Automated bookkeeping pipeline
"I can't find where my wheel loaders are" → Fleet tracking app
"I lost a contract because of poor documentation" → Tool for proposal and bid responses

## Framework 2: Productize Your Service

If you ALREADY sell a service, is there a productized version?

**Service:** "I do landscaping for 15 clients in my metro area"
**Product:** Route-planning and crew coordination tool for landscaping companies (RouteOptimizer)

**Service:** "I build websites for local businesses"
**Product:** Template/platform specifically for small service businesses

**Service:** "I help companies respond to government RFPs"
**Product:** SaaS tool that monitors procurement opportunities and flags matches

The pattern: you have domain expertise + customer contact. Productize what you already know.

## Framework 3: Picks and Shovels

During the gold rush, the gold miners didn't get rich. Levi's (jeans) and the shovel sellers got rich. Sell tools to people chasing the trend.

**Trend:** AI image generation is exploding
**Picks & shovels:** Billing/credit system for AI apps, prompt management, image hosting optimized for AI output

**Trend:** More small businesses going digital
**Picks & shovels:** OAuth/SSO integration as a service, QuickBooks plugins, Stripe payment solutions

**Trend:** Remote work becoming standard
**Picks & shovels:** Async video communication, virtual office tools, remote team-building

## Framework 4: Geographic Arbitrage

Something that exists in one market but not another. Or something that exists in major cities but not in smaller metros or rural areas.

Questions:
- Which SaaS tools do you use that lack localization for your market? (Language, auth integrations, payment processors, accounting software integration, local tax law compliance)
- Which service exists in big cities but not in your region?
- Which product works in one market but hasn't launched in another?

### Common localization opportunities:
- SSO/identity verification integration (anything that requires authentication)
- Accounting software ecosystems (plugins, integrations, automation for QuickBooks, Xero, etc.)
- Payment processing (checkout, invoicing, installment payments via Stripe, Square, etc.)
- Local regulatory compliance (procurement law, workplace safety, GDPR, tax codes)
- Region-specific workflow tools

## Framework 5: Boring Businesses

The sexiest businesses are the least profitable. The most boring businesses are often the most profitable. Nobody dreams of building invoicing software, but FreshBooks makes hundreds of millions.

Boring = good because:
- Low competition (nobody WANTS to build it)
- High willingness to pay (it solves a real pain)
- Low churn (switching costs are high)
- Predictable revenue (businesses need it every month)

Where to look:
- What do accountants, lawyers, dentists, plumbers complain about?
- What do small business owners still do on paper?
- What industry-specific software looks like it was built in 2005?
- What do people hire virtual assistants to do that software could handle?

### The "Would I be embarrassed at a dinner party?" test:
If the idea is boring enough that you'd hesitate to mention it at a dinner party — it's probably a great business. "I automate compliance reporting for dental offices" beats "I'm building an AI social network" every single day.

## Framework 6: Integration Plays

Two systems that don't talk to each other. You build the bridge.

- QuickBooks ↔ specific industry vertical software
- Google Calendar ↔ scheduling for contractors and tradespeople
- SSO provider ↔ any SaaS that needs enterprise authentication
- Stripe ↔ any e-commerce platform lacking native checkout
- Fleet GPS trackers ↔ customer reporting dashboards

Integration businesses have built-in distribution: both systems WANT the integration to exist and can help market it.

## Framework 7: Unbundle the Spreadsheet

Every industry has a critical spreadsheet that someone maintains. That spreadsheet IS the product — it just doesn't know it yet.

Signs of a spreadsheet waiting to become a product:
- Multiple people need access simultaneously
- It has manual formulas that break when someone edits the wrong cell
- Someone spends hours each week updating it
- It's emailed around as attachments
- It connects to other data sources via copy-paste

How to find them:
- Ask: "What's the most important spreadsheet in your business?"
- Look at QuickBooks exports that get manipulated in Excel before being useful
- Look at project management done in spreadsheets
- Look at inventory tracking done in spreadsheets

### Example:
"Every landscaping company tracks crew schedules, route assignments, and equipment allocation in a massive Google Sheet" → Purpose-built crew management SaaS for landscaping companies.

## Output Format

For each idea, provide:

1. **One-liner:** What is it in one sentence?
2. **Target customer:** WHO pays? Be specific. Not "small businesses" but "US-based landscaping companies with 5-20 crews"
3. **Problem:** What specific pain does it solve?
4. **Why you:** What advantage does the founder have?
5. **Initial distribution:** How do the first 10 customers find you?
6. **Revenue model:** How does the money flow?
7. **Gut check (1-10):** Honest first impression, where 1 = "this will never work" and 10 = "shut up and take my money"
