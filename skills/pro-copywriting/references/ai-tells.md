# AI Tells: Patterns That Scream "A Robot Wrote This"

These are the specific, identifiable patterns that make text recognizable as AI-generated. Not because they're grammatically wrong — they're usually perfectly correct. That's the problem. They're the verbal equivalent of `rounded-xl shadow-lg bg-gradient-to-r`.

## Opening Patterns

### The Setup Opening
AI almost always opens with context before content:

❌ "In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape, monitoring your infrastructure has never been more important."
✅ "Your server crashed at 3am. You found out from a tweet."

❌ "When it comes to choosing the perfect wedding cake, there are many factors to consider."
✅ "Cardamom and pear. That's the combination that made a bride cry at a tasting last March."

**The fix:** Delete your first sentence. Start with your second. If that's also setup, delete it too. Keep going until you hit something interesting.

### The "Whether You're..." Inclusive Opening
❌ "Whether you're a seasoned developer or just getting started, Pulseline helps you..."
❌ "Whether you're planning an intimate gathering or a grand celebration..."

This is AI hedging — trying to include everyone, addressing no one. A human writer picks a side.

✅ "You're running production. Things break. You need to know before your users do."
✅ "You want a cake that makes people put their phones down and pay attention."

### The Definition Opening
❌ "API observability is the practice of monitoring and understanding the behavior of your APIs in production environments."

Nobody googled your landing page to get a definition. They already know what the thing is. They want to know why YOUR thing is worth their time.

## Transition Patterns

### The Bridge Phrases
These are the AI equivalent of `<br/>` — structural padding that adds zero meaning:

- "But here's the thing..."
- "That being said..."
- "Here's where it gets interesting..."
- "The truth is..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "What sets us apart is..."
- "Let's dive in..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "Now, let's talk about..."
- "Moving on to..."
- "When it comes to..."

**The fix:** Delete the bridge phrase. Read the text without it. It almost always reads better. If the transition feels abrupt, the problem isn't the missing bridge — it's that the two paragraphs don't logically connect.

### The "Not Just X, But Y" Structure
❌ "It's not just a monitoring tool — it's a complete observability platform."
❌ "We don't just bake cakes — we create edible works of art."

This structure is the AI's favorite way to "add depth." It's become so overused that it now signals the opposite — shallow thinking dressed up as insight.

✅ "Monitoring tells you something broke. We tell you which deploy, which service, and which exact query did it."
✅ "The rosemary and lemon cake for the Andersson wedding took three recipe iterations. We bake until it's right."

## Vocabulary Patterns

### Buzzword Clusters
AI gravitates toward certain words so consistently that they've become markers:

**The Empowerment Cluster:**
leverage, unlock, empower, elevate, harness, supercharge, transform, revolutionize, game-changing, cutting-edge, next-level, state-of-the-art

**The Safety Cluster:**
comprehensive, robust, seamless, streamlined, holistic, end-to-end, world-class, best-in-class, industry-leading

**The Hedging Cluster:**
might, could, potentially, can help, aims to, strives to, is designed to, enables you to

**The fix:** Replace every buzzword with what you ACTUALLY mean:
- "Leverage AI" → "Use GPT-4 to sort your invoices"
- "Seamless integration" → "One line of code. No config file."
- "Comprehensive solution" → "Handles invoices, receipts, and payroll"
- "Elevate your experience" → (ask yourself what this even means, then write THAT)

### The Em-Dash Epidemic
This is one of the most recognizable AI tells in existence. AI uses em-dashes (—) constantly, often multiple times per paragraph, as a crutch for parenthetical asides, mid-sentence pivots, and dramatic pauses.

Real humans rarely write em-dashes in casual or commercial text. In Swedish, almost never. In English, sparingly in formal editorial writing, almost never in marketing copy, emails, or web text.

❌ "We monitor your APIs — all of them — in real time — so you never miss an incident."
✅ "We monitor all your APIs in real time. You won't miss an incident."

❌ "Varje tårta designas unik — för att det är ert bröllop — inte någon annans."
✅ "Varje tårta designas unik. Det är ert bröllop, inte någon annans."

**The fix:** Search your text for "—" (em-dash). For each one, replace with:
- A period and new sentence (most common fix)
- A comma (if it's a natural pause)
- Parentheses (if it's genuinely a parenthetical aside in formal text)
- Nothing (just merge the clauses)

A maximum of ONE em-dash per page of copy. Zero is better. If your text has 3+ em-dashes, it reads as AI-generated to anyone paying attention.

### The Adverb Problem
AI loves: truly, really, incredibly, remarkably, significantly, essentially, fundamentally, literally, simply, absolutely, undeniably

These words feel like emphasis but add nothing. "Incredibly fast" is less convincing than "47ms."

**The fix:** Delete the adverb. If the sentence feels weak without it, the problem is the sentence, not the missing adverb.

## Structure Patterns

### The Paragraph of Three
AI loves grouping things in threes, with each item getting exactly one sentence of description:

❌ "First, we monitor your endpoints. Second, we analyze the results. Third, we alert you when something goes wrong."

Real writing groups things by importance, not by count. Maybe one thing deserves three paragraphs and two others deserve a passing mention.

### The Symmetric List
Every bullet point the same length, the same structure, the same level of detail. AI treats lists as a grid — real writers treat them as a hierarchy where the most important item gets the most space.

### The Even Paragraph
Every paragraph is 3-4 sentences. Every sentence is 15-25 words. The text has no rhythm — just a steady hum. See `references/rhythm.md` for how to fix this.

### The Balanced Conclusion
❌ "While there are certainly challenges ahead, the potential benefits far outweigh the risks. By taking a thoughtful approach, you can..."

AI always hedges its conclusions. A human writer with conviction ends strong:
✅ "It works. Ship it."
✅ "Either your monitoring catches the problem or your customers do. Pick one."

## Tone Patterns

### Fake Enthusiasm
❌ "We're thrilled to announce..." / "We're excited to share..." / "We're passionate about..."

Nobody's thrilled. Show enthusiasm through what you DO, not by claiming to feel it.

### Corporate Warmth
❌ "We believe in putting our customers first."
❌ "At Flor, we're committed to quality."
❌ "Your satisfaction is our top priority."

This is the text equivalent of a stock photo smile. It says nothing because everyone says it.

✅ "Last Tuesday a customer called at 6pm asking if we could change the frosting flavor for a Saturday wedding. We did."

**The fix:** Replace every "we believe/we're committed" statement with a SPECIFIC EXAMPLE of that belief in action.

### The Diplomatic Non-Opinion
AI never takes a side. It presents "on one hand... on the other hand..." on every topic. This is appropriate for analysis. It's death for copywriting.

Good copy has a point of view:
✅ "Most monitoring tools are built for the company that sells them, not the engineer who uses them at 3am."
✅ "Fondant looks great in photos and tastes like sugary cardboard. We use buttercream."

## The Meta-Pattern: Predictability

The deepest AI tell isn't any single pattern — it's that you can PREDICT the next sentence. If you cover the second half of any paragraph and can guess roughly what it says, the writing is AI-generic.

Human writing surprises. Not with random nonsense — with specific details, unexpected angles, honest admissions, or bold claims that the reader didn't see coming.

**The test:** Read each paragraph. Can you predict the last sentence from the first? If yes, rewrite the last sentence to say something the reader wouldn't expect.
