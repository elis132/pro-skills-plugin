# Design Dimensions: Finding a Unique Position

Instead of picking from preset styles (which just gives you "AI Brutalist" instead of "AI Tailwind"), define your design as a unique position across independent dimensions. The combination of positions creates something that can't be templated.

## The Dimensions

### Formality Axis
How buttoned-up is this design?

`Raw/punk ←――――――→ Casual/friendly ←――――――→ Professional ←――――――→ Institutional/formal`

- **Raw**: Hand-drawn elements, visible construction, imperfect alignment, monospace type, exposed grid lines
- **Casual**: Rounded shapes, warm colors, playful type, generous spacing, conversational tone
- **Professional**: Clean grid, clear hierarchy, restrained palette, polished details, confident type
- **Institutional**: Serif type, structured layouts, muted colors, conventional patterns, gravitas

### Density Axis
How much breathing room exists?

`Extreme whitespace ←――――――→ Balanced ←――――――→ Editorial density ←――――――→ Information overload`

- **Extreme whitespace**: Japanese-influenced, zen, one element per viewport, silence as design
- **Balanced**: Comfortable reading, clear sections, standard content density
- **Editorial density**: Magazine-like, multiple elements competing for attention, rich layering
- **Information overload**: Dashboard/terminal aesthetic, every pixel used, designed density

### Temperature Axis
What emotional temperature?

`Cold/clinical ←――――――→ Neutral ←――――――→ Warm ←――――――→ Hot/passionate`

- **Cold**: Blue-grays, sharp edges, monospace, minimal, technical precision
- **Neutral**: Balanced palette, neither warm nor cold, functional
- **Warm**: Earth tones, serifs, natural textures, paper-like surfaces, human touch
- **Hot**: Saturated colors, bold type, high energy, dynamic layouts, urgency

### Era Reference Axis
What time period informs the aesthetic? (This is reference, not imitation)

`1950s Swiss ←→ 1970s print ←→ 1990s grunge ←→ Y2K digital ←→ 2010s flat ←→ 2020s neo-brutalism ←→ Near-future`

Each era brought specific aesthetic innovations:
- **1950s Swiss**: Grid discipline, Helvetica, primary colors, rational composition
- **1970s print**: Photo-typography, earth tones, experimental layouts, Letraset
- **1990s grunge**: Layered textures, distressed type, anti-design, chaos as aesthetic
- **Y2K**: Glossy surfaces, 3D elements, chrome, gradients, tech-optimism
- **2010s flat**: Minimal shadows, clean geometry, bright solid colors, card UI
- **Neo-brutalism**: Thick borders, solid shadows, monospace, raw markup, anti-polish
- **Near-future**: Glassmorphism, AI aesthetics reclaimed, spatial design, variable fonts

### Motion Philosophy
How does this design move?

`Static ←――――――→ Subtle transitions ←――――――→ Choreographed ←――――――→ Cinematic`

- **Static**: No animation. The design speaks through composition alone.
- **Subtle**: Hover states, smooth transitions, gentle fades. Motion serves function.
- **Choreographed**: Scroll-triggered animations, sequenced reveals, orchestrated experience.
- **Cinematic**: Full-screen transitions, parallax, video backgrounds, immersive storytelling.

### Geometry Axis
What shape language dominates?

`Geometric/angular ←――――――→ Mixed ←――――――→ Organic/curved`

- **Geometric**: Straight lines, sharp corners, strict grid, mathematical precision
- **Mixed**: Combines geometric structure with occasional organic elements
- **Organic**: Curved containers, blob shapes, flowing layouts, natural forms

## How to Use This

### Step 1: Position Intuitively
Based on the design brief, place yourself on each axis. Don't overthink — first instinct is usually right.

### Step 2: Check for Defaults
If your position is "center" on every axis, you've described the most generic design possible. Push at least 2-3 axes toward an extreme.

### Step 3: Check for Conflicts
Some combinations create interesting tension:
- Formal + Dense = Bloomberg terminal aesthetic
- Warm + Geometric = Mid-century modern
- Cold + Organic = Medical/scientific
- Raw + Extreme whitespace = Art gallery
- Hot + Geometric = Bauhaus poster
- Cold + Dense + Static = Infrastructure tool
- Warm + Organic + Cinematic = Luxury brand
- Raw + Hot + Choreographed = Music/festival
- Formal + Organic + Warm = High-end restaurant

Combinations that signal lazy thinking (not banned — but need extra justification):
- Casual + Warm + Organic = "friendly SaaS" (the default)
- Cold + Geometric + Minimal = "generic tech" (the default)
- Formal + Neutral + Balanced = "corporate nothing" (the default)
- Warm + Serif + Whitespace = "anti-AI tasteful" (the NEW default — watch out for this one)

**The most interesting designs have at least one CONTRADICTION** — one axis that goes against the rest. A warm, organic design with one element of cold geometric precision. A formal, structured layout with one burst of playful color. These contradictions are what make designs feel human.

### Step 4: Write It as a Sentence
Don't list axis values. Write a design direction that captures the FEELING:

**Instead of:** "Formality: casual, Density: whitespace, Temperature: warm, Era: 1970s, Motion: subtle, Geometry: organic"

**Write:** "A sun-faded bookshop that somehow has a website — warm paper textures, generous margins like a well-set book, serif typography that feels hand-picked, and the confidence to leave 60% of the screen empty. Movement is rare and gentle, like turning a page."

This sentence becomes the north star. Every design decision should feel like it belongs in this world.

## Example Directions for Common Requests

These are STARTING POINTS for brainstorming — not templates. Never copy one directly. And notice the RANGE: these aren't all "refined and minimal."

**"Build me a landing page for a developer tool"**
- OPTION A: "An architect's blueprint — precise, structured, light background like drafting paper, thin ruled lines, geometric sans, data-heavy. The tool is serious and the design respects that."
- OPTION B: "A neon terminal you actually want to stare at — dark but warm, with syntax-highlighted real code as the hero. Dense, opinionated, monospace-heavy. Feels like the tool is already running."
- OPTION C: "So minimal it's almost hostile — white page, one giant headline, a single pip install command, and nothing else above the fold. Confidence through absence."
- OPTION D: "A colorful playground — light background, saturated primary color blocks, playful illustrations, almost childlike simplicity. The contrast between the playful design and serious technical content IS the brand."

**"Build me a portfolio site"**
- OPTION A: "A museum catalog — editorial typography, each project gets its own spread, mostly monochrome."
- OPTION B: "A chaotic studio wall — overlapping images, handwritten notes, rotated elements, things pinned at angles. The mess IS the aesthetic."
- OPTION C: "A single page with nothing but images — full-bleed, edge-to-edge, no text visible until hover. The work speaks."
- OPTION D: "A brutalist directory — just a list of project names in monospace, each one a link. No thumbnails, no descriptions. Anti-portfolio as statement."

**"Build me a SaaS dashboard"**
- OPTION A: "Mission control — dark, dense, monospace numbers, subtle grid lines, color only for status."
- OPTION B: "A clean, bright workspace — white background, lots of air, soft colors, feels like a well-organized desk."
- OPTION C: "Bloomberg terminal meets modern design — extreme density, tabular data everywhere, zero decoration, maximum information."
- OPTION D: "Friendly and approachable — rounded elements, warm colors, illustrations for empty states, feels like a consumer app (even though it's B2B)."

**"Build me a site for a bakery/restaurant/food business"**
- OPTION A: "Moody food magazine — dark backgrounds, dramatic lighting, editorial serif typography."
- OPTION B: "Sunny kitchen energy — bright, warm yellows and whites, hand-drawn elements, photos that make you hungry, slightly chaotic layout like a recipe book."
- OPTION C: "The menu IS the site — typography-first, no images, elegant type treatment makes you imagine the food."
- OPTION D: "Instagram native — full-screen food photography grid, barely any text, tap/click to see details. The food is the design."

**"Build me a site for a wedding business"**
- OPTION A: "Dark, intimate, candlelit — moody photography, cream on near-black, serif italics."
- OPTION B: "Garden party — light, airy, floral watercolor accents, soft greens and pinks, organic shapes."
- OPTION C: "Fashion editorial — high contrast black and white, bold typography, dramatic poses, looks more like Vogue than a wedding vendor."
- OPTION D: "Scrapbook charm — textured paper backgrounds, mix of type styles, handwritten elements, photos with torn edges. Imperfect and personal."

## The Wildcard Mechanism

After establishing your direction, apply ONE wildcard — one element that doesn't "fit" the direction but creates surprise and memorability:

- A brutalist site with one section that's soft and organic
- A minimal site with one element that's oversized and bold
- A dark site with one section that's blindingly white
- A serious corporate site with one moment of humor
- A polished site with one intentionally rough/hand-drawn element
- A quiet site with one moment of animation that's elaborate

The wildcard prevents monotony. It's the thing people remember. It's what makes a designed site feel human rather than templated — because humans are inconsistent, and that inconsistency is charming.

**The wildcard should contrast with the dominant direction.** If the overall design is cold, the wildcard should be warm. If it's geometric, the wildcard should be organic. If it's minimal, the wildcard should be maximal. Tension creates interest.
